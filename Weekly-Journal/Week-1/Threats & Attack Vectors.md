Today, we cover the primary methods bad actors use to break your security. Understanding the "how" is the first step to building defenses.

#### **1. Common Threat Types: The "What"**

These are the categories of malicious activity.

- **Malware:** Malicious Software. This is an umbrella term.
    
    - _Examples:_ **Viruses** (attach to files), **Worms** (self-replicate across networks), **Trojans** (disguised as legitimate software), **Ransomware** (encrypts your data for payment), **Spyware** (secretly monitors you).
        
- **Social Engineering:** Manipulating people into breaking security procedures. It exploits human psychology, not software flaws.
    
    - _The principle:_ Why hack a system when you can hack a person?
        
- **Denial-of-Service (DoS/DDoS):** Overwhelming a system with traffic to crash it and disrupt **Availability**.
    
- **Phishing:** A subset of social engineering using fraudulent communications (emails, texts) to trick you into revealing information or downloading malware.
    

#### **2. Key Attack Vectors: The "How"**

This is _how_ the threats reach their target. Think of them as the delivery methods or entry points.

- **Email Attachments & Links:** The most common vector. A malicious file or link to a fake login page arrives in an inbox.
    
- **Software Vulnerabilities:** Flaws or bugs in code that attackers can exploit to gain unauthorized access. This is why software updates/patches are critical.
    
- **Weak or Stolen Credentials:** Using simple, reused, or leaked passwords to impersonate a legitimate user. This directly attacks **Confidentiality**.
    
- **Insider Threats:** Malicious or negligent actions by employees, contractors, or partners who have inside access.
    

---

**The Connection:** An **attack vector** (like a phishing email) is used to deliver a **threat** (like ransomware, which is a type of malware) to compromise the **CIA Triad** (by encrypting data, it destroys **Availability** and **Integrity**).

**Your Practical Takeaway for Today:**

1. **Look at your inbox.** Find a suspicious-looking email (maybe from a "shipping company" or "IT support"). Examine it for generic greetings, urgent language, and mismatched sender addresses. This is you learning to spot the vector.
    
2. **Think about your passwords.** Do you reuse them? This is the single biggest risk for most people. We'll tackle defense against this in a future lesson.
